About Me

Mechanical Engineer | Simulation, AI, and Breaking Things Virtually First

I'm a mechanical engineer who enjoys asking dangerous questions like, "What happens if this fails?" and then trying to answer them with simulations. I fell into the modeling world early on and learned two important lessons fast: models are always wrong, and some of them are still useful enough to save you a lot of time and money.

I've spent years working on aerospace, automotive, and heavy equipment problems, staring at stress plots, tuning assumptions, and convincing myself that this time the boundary conditions are totally reasonable. My goal has always been simple: break fewer real things by breaking more virtual ones first.

Lately, I've been poking around in AI and machine learning, mostly because I was skeptical and curious at the same time. I care about physics-based and model-driven AI, the kind that respects reality, behaves under pressure, and does not collapse the moment it sees messy data or a weird load case.

I like working where uncertainty lives. Where data is incomplete, decisions still have deadlines, and someone eventually has to say, "Yes, let's go with this." Turning that chaos into something usable is oddly satisfying.

These days I think a lot about systems, scale, and leadership, while staying close enough to the tech to ask annoying questions and catch bad ideas early. I still believe curiosity beats confidence, testing beats guessing, and coffee beats most problems.
